A thought experiment from Philipp

Imagine you had a time machine

Every breakthrough technology in history was once considered impossible — by smart people, with good reasons, who turned out to be wrong.

Philipp Samor von Holtzendorff-Fehling · Founder, Leela Quantum Tech
1700s
The thought experiment begins

If I had a time machine, and I brought someone back from the 1700s, and I sat them in front of a television — they would lose their mind.

Think about it. A glowing box showing moving images of people who aren't in the room. Voices coming from nowhere. Scenes from places they've never been, couldn't possibly travel to. They wouldn't just be confused. They might think I was the Devil. Their brain has no category for what they're seeing. No framework. No reference point. It's not that they're stupid — it's that the technology is so far beyond their reality that their mind literally cannot process it.

Their reaction
"Witchcraft. Sorcery. This is not possible. I need to leave this place."

We laugh at that. Of course a TV isn't magic. But put yourself in their shoes — they've never seen electricity, never heard a recorded sound, never seen a photograph. A television is so many leaps beyond their reality that every single step would need to be explained first.

1896
It happened for real

This isn't hypothetical. It actually happened.

In 1896, one of the first films ever shown to an audience was a simple clip: a train pulling into a station. The footage was shot from the platform. The train got bigger and bigger on the screen as it approached. And the audience — grown adults, educated people — jumped out of their chairs and ran for the exits.

They thought the train was going to come through the wall and kill them. A flat image on a white sheet made people flee for their lives. We laugh at them now. But their reaction was perfectly rational given what they knew. They had never seen moving images before. Their brain did the only logical thing: if something that looks like a train is coming at you, you move.

The lesson
"Every new technology looks like magic — or madness — until you've seen it work enough times that your brain creates a new category for it."
1960s
Now let's speed it up

Bring someone from the 1960s to today. Show them a modern TV. They won't think it's the Devil.

They've already seen televisions. They have a category for it. They'll be amazed at how thin it is, how sharp the picture is, how many channels exist. But they won't run screaming. Their brain can handle an improvement to something they already understand.

But now hand them a remote control.

Tell them: "Point this at the TV. Press this button. The channel changes. Wirelessly. From your couch."

Their reaction
"You're playing a prank on me. How is that possible? There's no wire. Nothing is connecting them. That's impossible."

The TV didn't break their brain. The remote control did. Because the TV was an improvement to something familiar. The remote control was a new principle — invisible energy traveling through the air, affecting a physical object, with no visible connection between them.

That's the exact reaction I get when I explain Quantum Upgrade.
