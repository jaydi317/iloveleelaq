Section 05 · Things you're going to love

Calibrated to your exact frequency. Every one of these is true.

Case · 01
Patient H.M.

Brain surgery in 1953 (for epilepsy) accidentally destroyed his ability to form new memories. For the next 55 years, every person he met became a stranger again five minutes later — even if he'd just talked to them. But his old memories stayed intact. He became the most-studied human in neuroscience history. When he died in 2008, his brain was sliced into 2,401 pieces and photographed. You can scroll through his actual brain online.

Case · 02
Alien Hand Syndrome

After certain brain surgeries, one hand starts acting on its own. It will unbutton a shirt the person just buttoned. One patient's hand tried to strangle them in their sleep. The conscious brain doesn't know what the hand is going to do next. Two wills, one body.

Case · 03
Split-Brain Patients

To treat severe epilepsy, surgeons used to cut the connection between the left and right halves of the brain. The result: each half became its own conscious person. The left half could talk. The right half couldn't. Patients literally argued with themselves. In one documented case, a patient's left hand punched him while his right hand tried to block it. Two people, one skull.

Case · 04
Capgras Delusion

Patients become convinced that their mom (or husband, or child) has been secretly replaced by an identical imposter. She looks like mom. She sounds like mom. They are certain she's a fake. The cause: a physical disconnection between the face-recognition part of the brain and the emotional-recognition part. They see mom but don't feel mom — so their brain concludes she must be a copy.

Case · 05
Wilder Penfield's Map

In the 1930s, a surgeon realized the brain itself has no pain receptors. So he began operating on awake patients and touching different spots on their brain with an electrode, asking what they felt. When he touched certain places, patients would suddenly smell their grandmother's kitchen, or hear a song from twenty years ago — full memories, triggered physically. He proved memory lives in specific physical locations by touching them.

Case · 06
Chuck Close

One of the most famous painters of the 20th century had face-blindness. He couldn't recognize faces — not even his wife's. That's why he painted giant portraits. His entire career was his attempt to understand what faces even are. A neurological deficit became world-class art.

Case · 07
Aaron Hernandez — the modern Phineas Gage

An NFL player who was convicted of murder and killed himself in prison at 27. When scientists autopsied his brain, they found Stage 3 CTE — brain damage from repeated head impacts — the worst ever seen in anyone his age. His prefrontal cortex was physically damaged. It's the exact same story as Phineas Gage, 175 years later: an injury to the front of the brain changed who a person was.
